    Benjamin Mako Hill : When Free Software Isn’t Better
    Advocates of “open source” cite the the power of “distributed peer review” as a mechanism by which software under free licenses will become better — in quality, reliability, and flexibility — than proprietary alternatives. He will use a close examination of free software projects and several large free software hosting communities to show how many free software projects are not better than proprietary alternatives. In terms of “distributed peer review”, He’ll show that most free software projects aren’t particularly collaborative either. He will also argue that, from the perspective of free software advocates, this is not bad news!

    Even when free software is not superior in ways that some past advocacy has focused on, free software offers a series of important philosophical and practical benefits over proprietary software. Moreover, by focusing on what free software sometimes doesn’t do well instead of only looking at our community’s wild successes, free software developers can build a better understanding of when communities coalesce around projects and when distributed peer review really does happen. This talk will suggest that rather than claiming that free software is better, we should instead set out to attempts to actually *make* it better.
     
    Karl-Heinz Strassemeier : Linux’tan Açık Kaynak Bulutuna,
    Linux’un yaratıcı ve etkili geliştirme ortamı, geleceğin verimli bilgi teknolojileri (IT) çözümleri için vazgeçilmez olan açık kaynak kod geliştirme kültürü için zemin hazırladı. Almanya’daki Lisog Derneği bu temel üzerine bir iş kültürü oluşturarak, katılımcı firmaların ortak olarak yenilikçi ve başarılı projeler geliştirmesini sağlıyor. Endüstrideki bulut akımı da, bu ortak proje yapma kültürünü destekliyor, IT servislerinin ulaştırılması için bir model sunuyor.

    Hans-Joachim Picht : From Penguins & Dinosaur’s: Linux on System z Install Lab

IBM System z also known as the mainframe is a 64bit high end system, with up to 96 processors at 5.2 Ghz and 3TB of memory. However, for many this is a new and strange environment. This workshop will provide an opportunity to install and configure Linux for System z in a virtual machine.
